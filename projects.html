Internships / Bachelors theses / Masters theses on The Grackle Project

The Grackle Project aims to understand how great-tailed grackles (Quiscalus mexicanus) use behavior to succeed in urban environments. The project began in 2012 and has collected extensive behavioral data in 4 populations across the US. We offer unpaid spring and summer internships and thesis projects for students at any level who are interested in animal behavior research and are skilled at detail-oriented work. As biologists, we understand the importance of diversity and we encourage applications from individuals belonging to groups that are traditionally underrepresented in the sciences.

Potential projects:
  1. Student-designed research in animal behavior: You are welcome to use our data to explore your own research questions. Check out the links above to get an idea of the data we have already collected and what we have done with it.

  2. Are populations on the range edge physiologically different? We collected physiological data (fat score and hematocrit [percentage of red blood cells]) for grackles at three latitudes (Tempe, Arizona; Woodland, California; and Sacramento, California). The student will extract data from various data sheets to make one data sheet, and then analyze the data using a statistical program of their choice to determine whether physiological variables vary with latitude.

  3. How widespread is male parental care? Great-tailed grackles are known to have a mating system where females are the sole builders of the nest and caretakers of the young. However, there is one report from Selander (1970) in which an unmarked male provided parental care to two juveniles in Austin, Texas. We are finding repeated parental care events by multiple individually marked adult males in Tempe, Arizona, and further events in Santa Barbara, California, and San Clemente, California (see Folsom et al. 2020 for more details in our preregistration). To understand how widespread male parental care might be in this species, the intern will search eBird.org for pictures of this species feeding their young. They will then enter data into a data sheet: the sex of the grackle that is giving the food, what the food is, and where the photo was taken. This project requires making a free profile at eBird.

  4. Does great- and boat-tailed grackle foraging behavior vary across their ranges? Great-tailed grackles (Quiscalus mexicanus) and boat-tailed grackles (Quiscalus major) are closely related species, however only the great-tailed grackle is rapidly expanding its geographic range. Could this be due to their different foraging pattens? The intern will begin to answer this question by searching eBird.org for pictures of each species eating. They will then enter data into a data sheet: the sex of the grackle that is eating the food, what the food is, and where the photo was taken. This project requires making a free profile at eBird. Note that this project does not have to cover both species - it can focus on only one species if that is preferable.

  5. Do great- and boat-tailed grackle foraging behaviors differ? Great-tailed grackles (Quiscalus mexicanus) and boat-tailed grackles (Quiscalus major) are closely related species, however only the great-tailed grackle is rapidly expanding its geographic range. Could this be due to their different foraging pattens? The intern will begin to answer this question by searching eBird.org for pictures of each species eating.
